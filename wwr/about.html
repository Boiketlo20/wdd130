History

Long before river rafting was a thrill-seeking adventure, it was a way of life. For centuries, the Indigenous peoples of the Missouri River, including the Mandan Tribe, were the original masters of its currents. The river was their highway, their pantry, and their heartline. The Mandan, who established large agricultural villages along the Upper Missouri, were renowned for their ingenuity. Their most distinctive river craft was the bull boat‚Äîa remarkable, circular vessel made by stretching a single buffalo hide over a sturdy frame of willow or cottonwood.

These agile, buoyant boats were ideal for transporting goods across the river and ferrying hides and crops for trade with neighboring tribes and, later, European explorers like Lewis and Clark, who relied on Mandan expertise to survive the winter of 1804. When you embark on a rafting trip today, you're participating in a timeless tradition. The sense of adventure and the profound respect for the river's power are a direct echo of the legacy left by the Mandan and other river peoples.
